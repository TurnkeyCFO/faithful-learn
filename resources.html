02 Primary Sources Read once before you set policy

The AI tools' own policies

Three sources

Before your church writes its own policy, it is worth reading what the tool-makers themselves permit and promise. These are the primary sources — acceptable-use terms and privacy commitments, written by the companies. They change; check the date when you read them.

Anthropic — usage & privacy policies Maker of Claude Anthropic's acceptable-use policy and privacy terms for Claude, including how input is handled across the consumer and business tiers. Visit anthropic.com OpenAI — usage & privacy policies Maker of ChatGPT OpenAI's usage policies and privacy terms for ChatGPT, including the difference between consumer accounts and the business and enterprise tiers. Visit openai.com Google — Gemini terms & AI principles Maker of Gemini Google's terms for Gemini and its published AI principles, including how Workspace data is treated differently from consumer accounts. 03 Further Reading Trusted outside sources

Research on technology & the church

Outside voices

We are one voice. These organizations study churches, ministry, and stewardship with more depth than any single resource can — worth knowing when a decision is significant.

Barna Group Church research Long-running research on faith, culture, and how churches and Christians relate to technology and change. Visit barna.com Lifeway Research Pastoral surveys Survey research on pastors and congregations, including recurring studies on the tools and pressures of ministry practice. Visit research.lifeway.com ECFA — Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability Stewardship & trust Guidance on integrity, governance, and the careful handling of information — a useful lens for the data questions AI raises. Visit ecfa.org Your denomination's own guidance Closest to home Many denominations and networks have begun issuing their own counsel on AI and ministry.
